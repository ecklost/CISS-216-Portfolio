What is Web Accessibility

With a websites potential to reach the entire world, website creators need to make their websites accessible to individuals with disabilities. The most stand-out type of disability for websites is visual, but disabilities come in different forms such as, but not limited to, auditory, physical, and cognitive. Even if people do not have life-long disabilities, a website should still be accessible for all individuals. This includes elderly people, people with slow connections or limited bandwidth, and those who are on mobile devices. A website should cater to as many groups as possible. Barring that, options should be readily available.

A Small List of Examples

  • Text Alternative
    • Description of images, charts, icons, etc.
    • Brief description of audio and visual files
  • Captions
    • Transcripts or captions for videos or radio interviews
    • Audio descriptions for important visual details
    • Sign language can also be used.
  • Functionality
    • Functionality of site that is equally available for mouse and keyboard
    • Keyboard support does not get trapped in the content
  • Time
    • Stop, extend, or adjust time limits when possible
    • Pause, stop, and hide scrolling content
    • Re-authenticate when a session expires without losing data.
